What is
Clean Water
and Sanitation
all About?

Clean water and sanitation are fundamental human rights and are essential for the health, dignity, and development of individuals and communities. Access to clean water means having safe, uncontaminated water available for drinking, cooking, and hygiene. Sanitation involves the proper management of human waste, wastewater, and hygiene practices, ensuring that environments remain clean and disease-free. The goal of clean water and sanitation is to prevent the spread of waterborne diseases, reduce environmental pollution, and improve the quality of life for people around the world.

Since the 1960s, about two-thirds of countries have experienced increasing water scarcity. This means that there's not enough water to meet everyone's needs. Over the past twenty years, we've seen some improvements, with more people having access to clean drinking water. But this progress is at risk because of rising demand and the unpredictable effects of climate change on our water sources.

That's why SDG 6, one of the United Nations' Sustainable Development Goals, focuses on ensuring that we have enough freshwater and using it wisely. This means finding ways to manage our water resources sustainably so that everyone has access to the water they need, now and in the future.

Despite global efforts to improve water and sanitation access, significant challenges persist in achieving SDG 6. Clean water and adequate sanitation are fundamental to health and development, yet over 2 billion people still face severe water scarcity, and billions lack safely managed sanitation services

Freshwater is a limited resource


While it may seem like there's plenty of water on Earth, the amount of freshwater we can actually use for drinking, irrigation, and other essential needs is limited. And even though we've made progress in providing clean water to more people, this progress is under threat due to population growth and climate change.

Challenges To Be Addressed

According to the United Nations Water Development Report 2023, over 2.2 billion people live in countries experiencing high water stress. By 2025, half of the world's population could be living in water-stressed areas. Water scarcity affects not only the access to drinking water but also food security, health, and economic development.

The World Health Organization (WHO) reports that 80% of wastewater globally is discharged untreated into the environment. Industrial activities, agricultural runoff, and sewage contribute to water pollution, leading to contamination of water bodies with harmful substances and pathogens.

The WHO/UNICEF Joint Monitoring Programme (JMP) reports that 3.6 billion people lack safely managed sanitation services. Poor sanitation is a major cause of diseases like cholera, dysentery, and typhoid. Inadequate sanitation affects public health, environmental quality, and human dignity.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) indicates that climate change is causing more frequent and severe droughts, floods, and extreme weather events. This impacts water availability, with some regions experiencing more intense water shortages and others facing increased flooding.

The UN estimates that 1.8 billion people globally still lack access to safely managed drinking water, and there are significant disparities between urban and rural areas. For example, in sub-Saharan Africa, over 400 million people rely on unsafe water sources.

According to the World Bank, many developing countries face challenges related to outdated or insufficient water infrastructure. The World Bank estimates that $114 billion is needed annually to achieve universal access to safe water and sanitation by 2030.

The UN estimates that financing for water and sanitation needs is significantly underfunded. The financing gap for water and sanitation infrastructure is projected to be $30 billion annually, which affects the ability to expand and maintain services.

The UN projects that the global population will reach 9.7 billion by 2050, with the majority living in urban areas. Rapid urbanization increases the demand for water and sanitation services, leading to overstressed resources and infrastructure.

The Global Public-Private Partnership for Handwashing (PPPHW) indicates that poor hygiene practices contribute to 1.7 million child deaths annually from diarrhea and respiratory infections. Education campaigns on water, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH) are crucial for improving public health outcomes.

TARGETS & OBJECTIVES

6.1

By 2030, achieve universal and equitable access to safe and affordable drinking water for all

6.2

By 2030, achieve access to adequate and equitable sanitation and hygiene for all and end open defecation, paying special attention to the needs of women and girls and those in vulnerable situations

6.3

By 2030, improve water quality by reducing pollution, eliminating dumping and minimizing release of hazardous chemicals and materials, halving the proportion of untreated wastewater and substantially increasing recycling and safe reuse globally

6.4

By 2030, substantially increase water-use efficiency across all sectors and ensure sustainable withdrawals and supply of freshwater to address water scarcity and substantially reduce the number of people suffering from water scarcity

6.5

By 2030, implement integrated water resources management at all levels, including through transboundary cooperation as appropriate

6.6

By 2020, protect and restore water-related ecosystems, including mountains, forests, wetlands, rivers, aquifers and lakes

6.a

By 2030, expand international cooperation and capacity-building support to developing countries in water- and sanitation-related activities and programmes, including water harvesting, desalination, water efficiency, wastewater treatment, recycling and reuse technologies

6.b

Support and strengthen the participation of local communities in improving water and sanitation management

Our Mission

Ensure access to water and sanitation for all. Access to safe water, sanitation and hygiene is the most basic human need for health and well-being. Billions of people will lack access to these basic services in 2030 unless progress quadruples.

Vision

SDG 6 focuses on ensuring a clean and stable water supply and effective water sanitation for all people by the year 2030. The goal is a reaction to the fact that many people throughout the world lack these basic services.

Our Approach

Clean Water and Sanitation is a crucial component of the United Nations' Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), focusing on ensuring the availability and sustainable management of water and sanitation for all. This goal recognizes the fundamental human right to access clean water and sanitation, which are essential for health, well-being, and economic prosperity. By addressing water scarcity, pollution, and inadequate sanitation, SDG 6 seeks to reduce the spread of diseases, improve child health and survival, increase productivity, promote gender equality, and protect the environment. Achieving this goal requires a multifaceted approach involving investment in infrastructure, sustainable water management practices, community engagement, and poverty reduction. Through collective action, governments, organizations, and communities can work together to create a world where everyone has access to clean water and sanitation, regardless of their circumstances.
